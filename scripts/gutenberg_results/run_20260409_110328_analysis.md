# Gutenberg 10-Novel Character Analysis: RLM Experiment Report

**Date:** April 9, 2026
**Model:** gpt-5.4 (root and sub-LM)
**Provider:** OpenAI
**Root iterations:** 3
**Total tokens:** 2,702,736 (2.57M input, 128K output)
**Result:** 10/10 novels successfully analyzed

## Problem Setup

We point a Recursive Language Model at 10 classic novels from Project Gutenberg -- 16MB of raw text spanning four centuries and five languages of origin -- and give it a single instruction: *For each novel, identify every named character, map their relationships to each other, and identify the central conflict. Which novel has the most complex character web?*

The RLM has no literary training data, no character databases, no NER models. It has six tools: `llm_query` (single-shot sub-LM call), `rlm_query` (spawn a full child RLM with its own REPL loop), `read_file`, `list_files`, `FINAL`, and `print`. Everything else -- the strategy, the chunking decisions, the JSON schemas, the alias resolution -- the model invents on its own.

The test is deliberately adversarial in scale. War and Peace is 3.4 million characters. Les Miserables is 3.4 million. Don Quixote is 2.4 million. Anna Karenina is 2.1 million. The model must figure out how to process texts that exceed typical working memory, decide when to delegate to children vs. handle directly, and produce structured output that can be programmatically compared across all ten novels.

## What the Model Did

The root LM wrote three code blocks total:

1. **Discovery** -- `list_files()` to see what's in the directory. Ten `.txt` files.

2. **Dispatch** -- A single for loop that reads each novel with `read_file()` and spawns a child RLM via `rlm_query()` with a detailed JSON schema for character extraction. Each child receives the full novel text as a context variable and independently decides how to analyze it.

3. **Synthesis** -- Parse all ten children's JSON results, compute per-novel metrics (character count, relationship count, complexity score), rank them, and call `FINAL()` with the combined structured result.

Each child RLM ran its own multi-iteration REPL loop. The typical pattern was:
- **Iteration 1:** Inspect the text -- print length, preview the opening, confirm the novel identity.
- **Iteration 2:** Strip Gutenberg headers/footers using exact marker strings (`*** START OF THE PROJECT GUTENBERG EBOOK ...`), then pass the cleaned text to `llm_query` with a strict JSON schema for character, relationship, and conflict extraction.
- **Iteration 3:** Parse the returned JSON, validate it, count characters and relationships, fix any structural issues.
- **Iteration 4:** Call `FINAL()` with the validated result.

This pattern was not prescribed. The system prompt says nothing about header stripping, JSON validation, or multi-pass extraction. The children independently converged on the same strategy.

## The Novels

### A Tale of Two Cities (1859) -- Charles Dickens

Dickens's novel of the French Revolution follows a network of characters split between London and Paris. Doctor Manette, released after eighteen years in the Bastille, is reunited with his daughter Lucie. Her marriage to Charles Darnay -- secretly a French aristocrat who has renounced his family name -- pulls the household into the Revolution's violence when Darnay returns to Paris and is condemned to die. Sydney Carton, a dissolute lawyer who loves Lucie, takes Darnay's place at the guillotine. Meanwhile, Madame Defarge knits her registry of aristocrats marked for death, her vengeance reaching beyond Darnay to anyone connected to the Evremonde name.

**34 characters, 40 relationships, complexity 5/10**

The model mapped the novel's characteristic dual structure: the London cluster (Lucie, Manette, Lorry, Pross, Carton) and the Paris cluster (the Defarges, the Evremonde legacy, the revolutionary mob). It correctly identified the hinge relationships that connect the two: Darnay's secret Evremonde identity linking him to both Manette's imprisonment and Madame Defarge's vendetta, and Defarge's former role as Manette's servant. The Carton-Darnay doubling is noted as both physical resemblance and romantic rivalry -- "They are physical doubles and romantic rivals; Carton ultimately dies in Darnay's place." Minor relationships are also tracked: Barsad and Cly as co-conspirators, Jerry Cruncher's family dynamics, Gabelle's plea that draws Darnay back to France. The model even picked up the Gaspard-Marquis thread -- the father who murders the aristocrat whose carriage killed his child.

### Anna Karenina (1878) -- Leo Tolstoy

Tolstoy's novel runs two parallel plots. Anna, married to the senior official Karenin, falls in love with the cavalry officer Vronsky. Their affair destroys her marriage, separates her from her son Seryozha, and ends in her suicide. In counterpoint, the landowner Levin courts Kitty Shcherbatskaya, marries her, and searches for meaning through farming, family, and a hard-won religious faith. Between them stands Stiva Oblonsky -- Anna's charming, faithless brother -- whose own marital crisis opens the novel and connects the two worlds.

**130 characters, 28 relationships, complexity 5/10**

The highest character count of any novel, reflecting the model's thorough sweep of Tolstoy's enormous cast. It identified all seven major figures (Anna, Vronsky, Karenin, Levin, Kitty, Stiva, Dolly) with full patronymics and aliases. The relationship map captures the novel's two-track structure: the Anna-Vronsky-Karenin triangle with its satellite figures (Countess Lidia Ivanovna's religious influence on Karenin, Princess Myakaya's blunt social commentary) and the Levin-Kitty marriage with its constellation of friends, rivals, and philosophical foils (Katavasov the scholar, Sviazhsky the liberal landowner, Veslovsky the flirt who provokes Levin's jealousy). The model correctly linked Dolly as the bridge between the two plots -- Anna's sister-in-law and Kitty's sister. Notably, it tracked Levin's relationship with his dying brother Nikolay and Nikolay's companion Marya Nikolaevna, a thread that deepens Levin's confrontation with mortality.

### Crime and Punishment (1866) -- Fyodor Dostoevsky

Raskolnikov, an impoverished former student in St. Petersburg, murders the pawnbroker Alyona Ivanovna and her half-sister Lizaveta to test his theory that extraordinary men may transgress moral law. Instead of liberation, the murders drive him into fever, paranoia, and a psychological duel with the investigating magistrate Porfiry Petrovich. His path toward confession runs through Sonya Marmeladova, a young woman forced into prostitution to support her family, whose faith becomes his moral lifeline. Around them orbit Raskolnikov's sister Dunya (pursued by the corrupt Svidrigailov and the mercenary Luzhin), his loyal friend Razumikhin, and the Marmeladov family in their cascade of poverty, drunkenness, and humiliation.

**42 characters, 46 relationships, complexity 9/10**

The model produced one of its densest relationship maps here. It correctly identified the novel's hub-and-spoke structure with Raskolnikov at the center, connected to nearly every other character through different relationship types: family (Dunya, Pulcheria Alexandrovna), spiritual (Sonya), intellectual adversary (Porfiry), dangerous mirror (Svidrigailov), loyal friendship (Razumikhin), enmity (Luzhin), and crime (Alyona, Lizaveta). The Russian patronymic handling is excellent -- "Avdotya Romanovna Raskolnikova (Dunya, Dounia, Avdotya Romanovna)" correctly unifies the character across her many name forms. The model also mapped the Marmeladov family's internal structure in detail: Semyon's alcoholism, Katerina Ivanovna's pride and desperation, Sonya's sacrifice, the children Polenka, Lida, and Kolya. Even peripheral legal figures are tracked -- Zametov at the police station, Nikodim Fomich, the false confessor Nikolai Dementyev.

### Don Quixote (1605/1615) -- Miguel de Cervantes

Cervantes's novel follows an aging gentleman who reads so many chivalric romances that he loses his mind, renames himself Don Quixote, and rides out to right wrongs and win glory for his imagined lady Dulcinea del Toboso. His squire Sancho Panza -- earthy, practical, credulous -- becomes his constant companion across two volumes of adventures: windmills mistaken for giants, inns mistaken for castles, puppet shows destroyed in chivalric fury, and an extended sojourn at a duke's palace where the pair become the subjects of elaborate aristocratic pranks. Interwoven are embedded novellas of love, betrayal, and captivity that expand the novel's world far beyond the knight and his squire.

**69 characters, 61 relationships, complexity 5/10**

Don Quixote poses a unique structural challenge: it is less a single plot than a picaresque anthology held together by its central pair. The model handled this well, identifying the core Don Quixote-Sancho partnership as the structural spine and then mapping the concentric circles around it: the village (Pero Perez the curate, Master Nicholas the barber, Samson Carrasco), the household (niece, housekeeper), the inn world (Juan Palomeque, Maritornes), the embedded love plots (Cardenio-Luscinda-Don Fernando-Dorothea, each with their own relationship web), the Duke and Duchess's elaborate theatrical cruelty, and the Part II characters like Roque Guinart the bandit, Don Antonio Moreno in Barcelona, and the Ricote-Ana Felix exile subplot. The model correctly identified Gines de Pasamonte's dual identity as Maese Pedro, and tracked the Marcela-Chrysostom episode as a standalone meditation on female autonomy.

### Great Expectations (1861) -- Charles Dickens

Pip, an orphan raised by his abusive sister and her gentle blacksmith husband Joe Gargery, is summoned as a boy to play at Satis House, the decaying mansion of the jilted Miss Havisham. There he falls in love with Estella, the beautiful girl Miss Havisham has raised to break men's hearts. When Pip receives "great expectations" from an anonymous benefactor, he assumes Miss Havisham intends him for Estella and moves to London to become a gentleman. The benefactor turns out to be Abel Magwitch, the convict Pip once helped as a terrified child on the marshes -- and Estella turns out to be Magwitch's daughter. The novel traces Pip's slow, painful education in the difference between social rank and moral worth.

**37 characters, 41 relationships, complexity 8/10**

The model gave this novel a high complexity score and for good reason: Great Expectations has one of the most intricately connected character webs in English fiction, where revelations in the final act rewrite the meaning of relationships established in the first. The model mapped the key structural links: Magwitch is Estella's father, Molly (Jaggers's housekeeper) is Estella's mother, Compeyson swindled Miss Havisham and partnered with Magwitch, and Jaggers is the legal node connecting all of them. It tracked the full Pip-Joe-Mrs. Joe-Biddy domestic cluster, the Satis House circle (Miss Havisham, Estella, the parasitic relatives Sarah Pocket, Camilla, Raymond), and Pip's London life (Herbert Pocket, Wemmick, Startop). The Orlick thread is correctly identified as enmity directed at both Pip and Mrs. Joe. Even the Wemmick-Miss Skiffins romance and Pip's secret financing of Herbert through Clarriker are captured.

### Les Miserables (1862) -- Victor Hugo

Hugo's novel follows Jean Valjean, released after nineteen years of imprisonment for stealing bread, as he is transformed by the mercy of Bishop Myriel and spends the rest of his life trying to live justly in a society that will not let him. As the factory owner Madeleine, he rescues the dying Fantine and promises to save her daughter Cosette from the Thenardiers. As Fauchelevent, he raises Cosette in hiding. As an aging man, he watches Cosette fall in love with the student Marius Pontmercy, saves Marius's life at the barricades of the 1832 June Rebellion, and yields Cosette to him before dying. Pursuing him throughout is Javert, the police inspector whose rigid devotion to law cannot accommodate the possibility that a convict might become a good man.

**83 characters, 58 relationships, complexity 5/10**

The model captured Hugo's characteristic breadth -- the novel spans criminal underworlds, religious communities, bourgeois households, student revolutionaries, and Napoleonic history. It mapped all the key relationship layers: Valjean's chain of identity transformations and the people who know him under each name, the Thenardier family as a persistent antagonist thread (Thenardier, his wife, Eponine, Gavroche, Azelma), the Friends of the ABC revolutionary circle (Enjolras, Combeferre, Courfeyrac, Feuilly, Bahorel, Bossuet, Joly, Grantaire, Jean Prouvaire), and the Pontmercy-Gillenormand generational conflict. The Fantine thread is traced from her seduction by Tholomyes through her exploitation by the Thenardiers to her rescue by Valjean. The Patron-Minette criminal network (Claquesous, Montparnasse, Babet, Gueulemer, Brujon) is correctly associated with Thenardier. The Champmathieu trial -- where Valjean reveals his identity to save an innocent man -- is captured as a relationship between Valjean and Champmathieu. Even minor connective figures like Mabeuf (who links Marius to his father's memory) and Toussaint (Valjean's loyal servant) are included.

### Moby-Dick (1851) -- Herman Melville

Ishmael ships aboard the Pequod, a Nantucket whaling vessel commanded by Captain Ahab, who has lost a leg to the white whale Moby Dick and has bent his entire voyage toward killing it. The ship's officers -- the prudent Starbuck, the jovial Stubb, the pugnacious Flask -- and its multinational crew of harpooneers are drawn into Ahab's obsession despite their own better judgment. The novel alternates between the forward momentum of the hunt and Ishmael's encyclopedic digressions on whaling, cetology, and the metaphysics of whiteness. In the final chase, Moby Dick destroys the Pequod and kills everyone aboard except Ishmael.

**65 characters, 59 relationships, complexity 5/10**

The model mapped the Pequod's social hierarchy with precision: Ahab at the top, the three mates and their assigned harpooneers (Starbuck-Queequeg, Stubb-Tashtego, Flask-Daggoo), and the supporting crew. It captured the Ahab-Moby Dick relationship as mutual enmity, and the Ahab-Starbuck tension as the novel's moral center -- "Starbuck serves as Ahab's chief mate but morally resists his monomania." The shore relationships are tracked: Peleg and Bildad as co-owners, Peter Coffin at the Spouter-Inn, Father Mapple's sermon as mentorship to Ishmael, Ahab's unnamed wife and child waiting ashore. The model also followed the novel's gam encounters with other ships: Captain Boomer of the Samuel Enderby (who also lost a limb to Moby Dick but chose to let it go), Captain Mayhew of the Jeroboam and the fanatical Gabriel, the Steelkilt-Radney mutiny story from the Town-Ho. Even the Ishmael-Queequeg friendship -- "They become intimate companions and shipmates, calling each other bosom friends" -- is correctly identified as the novel's emotional anchor alongside the Ahab obsession.

### Pride and Prejudice (1813) -- Jane Austen

The five Bennet sisters live in rural Hertfordshire under the shadow of an entail that will leave them homeless when their father dies. When the wealthy Mr. Bingley takes Netherfield Park, Mrs. Bennet launches her campaign to marry her daughters advantageously. Jane and Bingley fall quietly in love. Elizabeth and Darcy circle each other through pride, prejudice, and misunderstanding before arriving at mutual respect and love. Wickham charms Elizabeth with false tales of Darcy's cruelty, then elopes with Lydia in a scandal that nearly ruins the family. Collins, the obsequious clergyman who will inherit the estate, proposes to Elizabeth, is rejected, and marries her friend Charlotte Lucas instead.

**52 characters, 57 relationships, complexity 9/10**

The model gave Pride and Prejudice its highest complexity score among novels with fewer than 60 characters, recognizing that Austen's web is dense rather than sprawling -- almost every character has a direct plot-relevant connection to multiple others. The relationship map captures the full Bennet family matrix (Mr. and Mrs. Bennet's mismatched marriage, Elizabeth as her father's favorite, Mrs. Bennet's indulgence of Lydia), the Bingley circle (Caroline's enmity toward Jane and Elizabeth, Louisa Hurst's alliance with her sister), the Darcy network (Georgiana, Colonel Fitzwilliam, Lady Catherine, Anne de Bourgh), the Wickham thread (his attempted elopement with Georgiana, his seduction of Lydia, Mrs. Younge as his accomplice), and the Collins-Charlotte-Lady Catherine patronage chain. The Gardiner couple is correctly identified as a stabilizing force -- sensible, affectionate, and instrumental in resolving the Lydia crisis. The model even tracked Mrs. Reynolds, Pemberley's housekeeper, whose praise of Darcy helps shift Elizabeth's opinion.

### The Odyssey (~8th century BCE) -- Homer

Odysseus has been trying to reach Ithaca for ten years since the fall of Troy. Poseidon persecutes him for blinding the Cyclops Polyphemus. Calypso detains him on her island. Circe transforms his men into pigs. He descends to the underworld to consult the dead. Meanwhile, in Ithaca, his wife Penelope fends off a mob of suitors who feast in his hall and plot to murder his son Telemachus. Athena guides both father and son -- Telemachus on a journey to Pylos and Sparta to seek news, Odysseus through his final trials -- until they reunite, slaughter the suitors, and restore the household.

**82 characters, 47 relationships, complexity 9/10**

The model handled the poem's multi-layered structure: the Ithaca plot (Penelope, Telemachus, the suitors, the loyal servants), the wanderings (Calypso, Circe, Polyphemus, Alcinous and the Phaeacians), the divine machinery (Athena's patronage, Poseidon's persecution, Zeus's arbitration), and the inset narratives (Agamemnon-Clytemnestra-Orestes as a dark mirror, Menelaus and Helen reunited at Sparta). The suitor relationships are mapped through Antinous as the violent aggressor and Eurymachus as the smooth manipulator, both opposed to Telemachus. The servant loyalty network is tracked through Eumaeus (the swineherd), Eurycleia (the old nurse), and their contrast with the disloyal Melanthius. The divine relationships form their own web: Athena petitioning Zeus, Zeus dispatching Hermes to Calypso, Poseidon opposed to Athena. The model even caught the Argos moment -- Odysseus's old dog recognizing him at his return, filed simply as "friendship."

### War and Peace (1869) -- Leo Tolstoy

Tolstoy's novel follows three aristocratic families -- the Rostovs, the Bolkonskys, and the Bezukhovs -- through the Napoleonic Wars from 1805 to 1812 and their aftermath. Pierre Bezukhov, illegitimate heir to a vast fortune, stumbles through a disastrous marriage to Helene Kuragina, a Masonic phase, and wartime captivity before finding meaning in marriage to Natasha Rostova. Prince Andrei Bolkonsky searches for glory at Austerlitz, finds disillusionment, falls in love with Natasha, loses her to Anatole Kuragin's seduction, and dies of his wounds after Borodino. Nikolai Rostov serves as a cavalry officer, gambles ruinously, and eventually marries Princess Marya Bolkonskaya. Behind them all, Kutuzov wages patient strategic defense against Napoleon's invading army.

**80 characters, 62 relationships, complexity 10/10**

The only novel rated 10/10. The model mapped the full three-family structure and the threads that bind them: Pierre-Andrei friendship, Andrei-Natasha engagement, Nikolai-Marya marriage, the Kuragin family as agents of disruption across all three households (Helene's destructive marriage to Pierre, Anatole's seduction of Natasha, Prince Vasili's scheming for Marya's fortune). The relationship web spans domestic and historical scales simultaneously: Pierre's philosophical mentors (Bazdeev the Mason, Platon Karataev the peasant), Andrei's military career under Kutuzov, the Rostov household's warmth and financial ruin, the old Prince Bolkonsky's intellectual tyranny over Marya. The model tracked Anna Pavlovna Scherer's salon as a social node where characters first meet and schemes are hatched, Dolokhov as a destabilizing force who duels Pierre, fleeces Nikolai at cards, and proposes to Sonya. Even the war layer is connected: Kutuzov-Bagration at the military level, Napoleon-Alexander I at the sovereign level, Petya Rostov's death in Denisov's partisan detachment connecting the domestic and military narratives.

## The Verdict

The model's answer: **War and Peace** has the most complex character web.

Its justification: "The novel features a very large cast spanning multiple aristocratic families, military commands, salons, estates, and political circles, with dense interconnections across family, romance, friendship, inheritance, philosophy, and war. Major figures are linked through overlapping domestic and historical networks, making the social web exceptionally broad and intricate."

War and Peace wins not by raw character count (Anna Karenina has more named characters) but by relationship density and structural complexity. Its 62 relationships span more relationship types and connect more distinct social spheres than any other novel. The three-family structure means that almost every major character has meaningful connections to characters in the other families, creating a web where removing any single node would disrupt multiple storylines.

The complexity scores reveal the model's judgment about interconnection vs. enumeration. Anna Karenina has 130 characters but only 28 mapped relationships and a 5/10 complexity score -- many of those 130 are minor salon figures without dense mutual connections. Pride and Prejudice has only 52 characters but scores 9/10 because almost every one of them has a direct, plot-relevant connection to multiple others. Crime and Punishment scores 9/10 for its hub-and-spoke intensity: Raskolnikov touches nearly every character through a different relationship type.

## Emergent Strategies

Every strategy below was invented by the model. The system prompt provides tools but no instructions about how to use them for literary analysis.

**Gutenberg header stripping.** Every child independently identified and removed Project Gutenberg metadata before analysis. Most used exact string matching on the `*** START OF` / `*** END OF` markers, some with novel-specific titles in the marker. No child attempted to analyze the metadata as part of the novel.

**Single-shot extraction with strict JSON schema.** Rather than making multiple LLM calls per novel, most children passed the entire cleaned text (up to 3.4M characters) in a single `llm_query` call with a detailed JSON schema specifying the exact output format. This trusts the sub-model's large context window rather than chunking.

**Validation and self-repair.** After receiving JSON from the sub-model, children parsed it, counted characters and relationships, and checked for structural issues. When parsing failed, the root used `llm_query` as a JSON repair tool: "Convert this to valid JSON only, preserving content exactly as much as possible."

**Regex-based name frequency analysis.** For Russian novels with patronymics and multiple name forms, children wrote regex to extract multi-word capitalized names and build frequency tables before LLM extraction, giving the sub-model a candidate list to work with.

**Alias resolution across naming conventions.** The model consistently unified characters across their various name forms. Russian patronymics (Avdotya Romanovna / Dunya / Dounia), Spanish honorifics (Don Quixote / Alonso Quixano / The Knight of the Rueful Countenance), Homeric epithets (Odysseus / son of Laertes / man of many wiles), and Dickensian pseudonyms (Jean Valjean / Madeleine / Fauchelevent / Leblanc) are all tracked.

**Relationship typing.** Without being told what types to use, the model consistently applied a taxonomy of relationship types: family, romantic, friendship, enmity, mentorship, social, professional, other. This emerged from the JSON schema the root defined in its dispatch code.
